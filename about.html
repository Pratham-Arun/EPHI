About Exoplanets

Understanding the science behind planetary habitability and the search for life beyond Earth

What Are Exoplanets?

An exoplanet is a planet that orbits a star outside our Solar System. These worlds come in an extraordinary variety — from scorching hot gas giants to rocky planets that may harbor liquid water on their surfaces.

Since the first confirmed exoplanet discovery in 1992, astronomers have identified over 6,000 exoplanets using techniques such as the transit method and radial velocity measurements. Each discovery brings us closer to answering the profound question: are we alone in the universe?

The study of exoplanets combines multiple disciplines including astrophysics, planetary science, and atmospheric chemistry. Modern telescopes can now detect the chemical signatures of exoplanet atmospheres, opening new frontiers in the search for biosignatures.

🔭

Key Discovery Methods

  • Transit Method
    Detects planets by measuring periodic dips in starlight
  • Radial Velocity
    Measures the star's wobble caused by a planet's gravitational pull
  • Direct Imaging
    Captures light directly from the planet itself
  • Gravitational Microlensing
    Uses gravity of stars to bend and magnify light from distant planets

The Habitable Zone

Understanding the "Goldilocks Zone" where conditions may be just right for life

🔥

Too Hot

Planets too close to their star receive excessive radiation. Surface temperatures can exceed 400°C, causing any water to evaporate. Venus in our solar system is an example.

Inner Boundary
💧

Just Right

The habitable zone — the distance from a star where liquid water could exist on a planet's surface. Temperature, atmospheric pressure, and stellar luminosity all play crucial roles.

Habitable Zone
❄️

Too Cold

Planets too far from their star receive insufficient energy. Any surface water would freeze. Mars exists near the outer edge of our Sun's habitable zone.

Outer Boundary

Key Space Missions

The missions that have revolutionized our understanding of exoplanets

2009 — 2018

Kepler Space Telescope

Kepler was NASA's first dedicated exoplanet-hunting mission. It observed over 530,000 stars and discovered more than 2,600 confirmed exoplanets. Kepler demonstrated that planets are common in our galaxy, with some estimates suggesting more planets than stars exist in the Milky Way.

2,600+ Planets
530K Stars
2018 — Present

TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite)

TESS is surveying over 200,000 of the brightest stars near Earth to search for transiting exoplanets. Unlike Kepler, TESS examines stars across the entire sky. It has already identified thousands of planet candidates, many of which are in the habitable zones of their stars.
